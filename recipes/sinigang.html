Sinigang

Description:

A true Filipino dish can be traced back even before the spanish came to the Philippines, as suggested by food historians it origins are most likely indigenous hence there is no documentation of what and where the origin of sinigang is. It's believed to have originated in the Philippines during the pre-colonial era. The indigenous people of the Philippines have been using tamarind as a souring agent for their dishes for centuries. It's likely that the dish evolved over time, with different regions and ethnic groups putting their own spin on it. Sinigang is more than just a dish, it's an integral part of the Filipino culture.

Sinigang is a staple dish in Filipino cuisine and it is known for its sour and savory taste. One of the things that makes Sinigang unique is the use of tamarind as a souring agent. It rouses the senses because of the sourness and slight spice. You can serve it with rice and it is a complete meal of its own. There are, of course, different ways to prepare it and has a lot of variations like sinigang na hipon, sinigang na isda, and sinigang sa pakwan.

Ingredients:

  • 1 tbsp oil of your choice
  • 3lbs pork spareribs, cubed
  • 1 large onion, quartered
  • 3 large tomatoes, quartered
  • 1.8 oz(pack) frozen kamias
  • 6 cups of water
  • 4 pieces gabi (or taro), peeled and halved
  • 1 bunch kangkong or bokchoy or spinach
  • fish sauce to taste

Steps:

  1. In a pot over medium heat, add oil and sear pork ribs, 1-2 minutes each side. Remove and set aside.
  2. In the same pot, add onion and tomato. Saute for 3-4 minutes until onions begin to brown.
  3. Add frozen kamias and return pork ribs to the pot. Add water and bring to a boil.
  4. Reduce heat, cover and cook for 60 minutes until pork is fork tender.
  5. Add gabi and cook for 10 minutes.
  6. Add kangkong and cook for another 2 minutes.
  7. Remove from heat and serve hot.
